# Action Center Standalone Readiness And Commercialization Hardening

## Status
Proposed

## 1. Purpose

This spec defines the **near-standalone enterprise and commercial readiness layer for Action Center**.

The existing maturity roadmap remains the strategic route:

1. Proven Embedded Enterprise Module
2. Controlled Cross-Route Platform
3. Standalone Bounded Follow-Through Product

This spec does not replace that roadmap.

It hardens the missing layer above it:

- category readiness
- commercial readiness
- operating readiness
- enterprise-governance readiness

This spec does **not**:

- authorize standalone launch
- authorize route expansion
- authorize a separate go-to-market motion
- authorize standalone pricing
- claim adoption proof
- claim causal intervention impact

Its purpose is to prepare Action Center to become **nearly standalone-explainable and enterprise-defensible** while preserving current product truth:

- Action Center remains embedded in Loep / Verisight for now
- approved route families remain `exit` and `retention`
- bounded route eligibility remains intact
- standalone launch remains a later gated possibility

## 2. Product Position

### 2.1 Current product position

Action Center is currently:

- embedded in Loep / Verisight
- attached to approved route families
- used to govern follow-through after a structured people-insight route
- bounded to `route -> action -> review -> closeout / continuation`
- HR-governed and manager-light
- canonical inside Action Center
- bounded in evidence, review rhythm, and continuation logic

### 2.2 Target readiness position

Action Center should become:

- commercially understandable as its own serious product layer
- enterprise-governable without relying on Loep-specific assumptions
- strong enough to support future standalone consideration
- still bounded, non-generic, and route-driven

This target readiness position still does **not** mean:

- standalone launch readiness by default
- route expansion by default
- broad workflow positioning
- broad HR suite positioning

## 3. Category Definition

### 3.1 Category name

**Governed Follow-Through**

### 3.2 Buyer problem

Organizations often generate people insights, route signals, or post-scan management conclusions, but the follow-through disappears into:

- email
- spreadsheets
- meetings
- undocumented owner agreements
- missed review moments
- weak closeout discipline

### 3.3 Replacement narrative

Action Center does not replace:

- Asana
- Monday
- Jira
- ServiceNow
- HRIS platforms
- survey platforms

It replaces or reduces dependence on:

- loose follow-up emails
- Excel action lists
- forgotten review moments
- vague ownership after reports
- non-auditable management agreements
- HR manually chasing every next step
- reporting outputs that do not become governed follow-through

### 3.4 Core promise

Action Center is a governed follow-through layer for turning structured people-insight routes into owned action, review rhythm, and closeout.

### 3.5 What existing tools fail to solve

Existing tools often fail because they are either:

- too broad, becoming project or workflow tooling
- too passive, stopping at reporting and dashboards
- too person-centric, drifting toward monitoring or dossier behavior
- too weak on review rhythm, governance, and closeout discipline

### 3.6 Why boundedness is a strength

Boundedness is a strength because it:

- preserves route truth
- preserves HR governance
- limits scope drift
- reduces privacy and dossier risk
- keeps manager participation light
- creates a category distinct from generic action planning

Action Center is not generic action planning.
It is not workflow management.
It is not project management.
It is not a survey dashboard.

It is **governed follow-through after structured people routes**.

## 4. Buyer Problem And Replacement Narrative

Action Center solves a narrow but recurring enterprise problem:

> insights and route outcomes exist, but disciplined follow-through disappears before it becomes governable, reviewable, and closeable.

It does not replace horizontal systems.

It replaces or reduces dependence on fragmented follow-through habits such as:

- follow-up living in inboxes
- route ownership becoming vague after reporting
- review moments being forgotten or improvised
- HR needing to chase managers manually
- management agreements becoming non-auditable
- route outputs failing to become governed next steps

Commercially, this means Action Center should be understandable as:

- not another planning tool
- not another survey dashboard
- but the layer that makes structured people-route follow-through visible, owned, reviewed, and closed out

## 5. ICP For Near-Standalone Readiness

### 5.1 Ideal customer profile

Action Center is most credible in organizations that have:

- recurring people-insight routes or recurring HR / management scans
- an HR team with clear governance ownership after reporting
- layered management structures where ownership often disappears after reports or debriefs
- visible follow-through leakage after insights are delivered
- a need for governance without rolling out a broad project-management tool
- sensitivity to privacy-safe, route-bound follow-through
- a model where HR remains rhythm owner and managers participate lightly
- enough operating maturity to engage with governance, privacy, and sign-off discipline

### 5.1A ICP qualifiers for near-standalone readiness

Near-standalone Action Center is especially credible where the customer has most of the following:

- multiple management layers
- recurring route cadence rather than one-off insight work
- central HR governance ownership
- a real review rhythm after reporting
- repeated evidence that insight follow-through leaks into email or meetings
- low appetite for broad workflow tooling rollout
- willingness to adopt explicit privacy and governance boundaries

### 5.2 Non-ICP

Action Center is not a fit for organizations that want:

- a generic task board
- employee monitoring
- broad HR case management
- follow-through without clear ownership or review rhythm
- causal proof of intervention impact as the main promise
- no clear HR governance owner
- fully autonomous manager execution without governance checkpoints
- task administration as the primary user behavior

## 6. Commercial Packaging Ladder

### 6.1 Level 1 - Embedded Action Center

**What is sold**

- Action Center attached to `ExitScan` / `RetentieScan`
- sold as part of or add-on to approved scan routes

**Buyer promise**

- structured route outcomes become owned, reviewed, and governable follow-through

**What is not sold**

- no standalone promise
- no cross-route platform promise
- no workflow or project-management layer

**Activation gate**

- available only where the approved route family already exists

**Pricing implication**

- should be priced as embedded route value, add-on value, or governed follow-through extension

**Risk if sold too early**

- buyers may mistake it for a light feature instead of a serious operating layer

**Sales motion boundaries**

| Dimension | Embedded Action Center |
| --- | --- |
| Buyer | HR buyer, HR route owner, management sponsor tied to the route |
| Sales motion | route-led or module-led sale |
| Demo focus | route -> action -> review -> closeout inside one approved route family |
| Main objection | "Is this just a small follow-up feature?" |
| Allowed sales language | governed follow-through for approved routes |
| Must not claim | standalone capability, generic workflow replacement, universal route fit |

### 6.2 Level 2 - Cross-Route Action Center

**What is sold**

- Action Center available across multiple approved route families

**Buyer promise**

- one governed follow-through layer across multiple structured people routes

**What is not sold**

- no generic workflow promise
- no universal fit claim across all modules

**Activation gate**

- requires route eligibility contract and live evidence per approved route family

**Pricing implication**

- should be positionable as cross-route governance value, not as horizontal productivity software

**Risk if sold too early**

- route families may look artificially compatible when they are not

**Sales motion boundaries**

| Dimension | Cross-Route Action Center |
| --- | --- |
| Buyer | HR governance owner, enterprise sponsor, route portfolio owner |
| Sales motion | multi-route governance sale inside Loep context |
| Demo focus | one shared follow-through layer across multiple approved route types |
| Main objection | "Will this become another workflow layer?" |
| Allowed sales language | governed follow-through across approved structured people routes |
| Must not claim | generic route support, automatic module fit, standalone product status |

### 6.3 Level 3 - Standalone Action Center

**What is sold**

- future-gated possibility only
- Action Center as its own governed follow-through layer around approved structured people routes

**Buyer promise**

- governed follow-through as an enterprise layer that sits after structured people routes and preserves ownership, review rhythm, and closeout

**What is not sold**

- not a generic action-planning suite
- not a broad workflow system
- not broad HR operating infrastructure

**Activation gate**

- requires universal route contract, tenant-grade governance, onboarding model, support model, commercial validation, and live evidence

**Pricing implication**

- may later justify standalone packaging, but this spec does not authorize standalone price setting

**Risk if sold too early**

- category confusion
- workflow misclassification
- insufficient governance/admin readiness
- standalone promise without live evidence

**Sales motion boundaries**

| Dimension | Standalone Action Center |
| --- | --- |
| Buyer | future-gated only |
| Sales motion | not yet authorized |
| Demo focus | not yet authorized as a standalone motion |
| Main objection | "Why is this not just workflow software?" |
| Allowed sales language | future bounded standalone possibility only |
| Must not claim | launch readiness, standalone pricing, standalone GTM, universal route support |

### 6.4 Packaging readiness status

| Packaging level | Current status | Allowed use now | Not allowed now |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `Embedded Action Center` | usable / near-ready | route-bound embedded or add-on positioning inside approved routes | standalone framing |
| `Cross-Route Action Center` | strategic direction / partial | internal product direction and controlled enterprise framing | broad buyer promise without route evidence |
| `Standalone Action Center` | not ready | future gated possibility only | launch, pricing, GTM, universal route promise |

## 7. Tenant And Admin Readiness

Near-standalone readiness eventually requires tenant-grade governance and administration.

At minimum, that must include:

- tenant admin role
- HR governance admin role
- manager participant role
- executive viewer role
- Verisight / support operator role
- role-based visibility
- route activation approvals
- route deactivation / archival controls
- audit export
- support access logging
- data retention controls
- deletion / archival policy
- incident process
- environment separation where relevant

This spec does not implement these controls.

It defines them as **standalone-readiness requirements**.

## 8. Universal Route Operating Contract

Route eligibility decides whether a route may enter Action Center.

The **route operating contract** defines how a route behaves once admitted.

Every route must provide:

- route family
- route intent
- bounded management question
- route owner
- manager participant model
- default cadence
- action policy
- evidence policy
- review policy
- closeout policy
- continuation policy
- governance queues
- reporting semantics
- privacy / dossier boundaries
- route activation checklist
- route deactivation / retirement rule

### 8.1 Minimum viable route package

Every route family that is allowed to operate inside Action Center must also have a minimum viable route package containing:

- route definition
- buyer-facing route intent
- default cadence
- allowed action focus
- evidence grammar
- closeout question
- governance queues
- route-specific red flags
- reporting vocabulary
- privacy boundary
- demo scenario

### 8.2 Key distinction

- **Route eligibility** answers: may this route enter?
- **Route operating contract** answers: how does this route behave once admitted?

## 9. Adapter Governance

Route adapters may vary:

- default cadence
- route-specific closeout questions
- evidence expectations
- action focus
- route labels
- readback interpretation

Route adapters may **not** vary:

- canonical route truth
- action-card contract core
- HR final closeout authority
- off-platform canonical write rules
- privacy / dossier boundaries
- workflow broadening rules
- manager-light principle

### 9.1 Adapter approval requirements

- product owner approval required
- governance/trust review required
- regression tests required
- route-fit score required
- live evidence required before broad activation

## 10. Support And Onboarding Model

Near-standalone readiness requires an operating model that does not depend on founder explanation or ad hoc setup.

It must eventually define:

- customer onboarding flow
- HR operator onboarding
- manager participation onboarding
- route activation checklist
- support escalation model
- governance incident handling
- privacy/dossier question handling
- failed rollout handling
- customer success review rhythm
- evidence review after first live cycle

### 10.1 Minimum near-standalone operating requirements

- HR operator onboarding should be repeatable in a short, documented session
- manager participation onboarding should be explainable through a compact artifact or lightweight intro
- first route activation should require a checklist rather than tacit knowledge
- support should distinguish between product issue, governance incident, privacy question, and route-semantics issue
- failed rollout handling should include containment and next-step logic

Action Center cannot become near-standalone ready if:

- onboarding depends on tacit founder context
- support depends on route-specific heroics
- governance incidents require undocumented judgment calls

## 11. Standalone Readiness Checklist

### 11.1 Product readiness

- requirement: stable bounded execution, governance, and readback truth
- evidence needed: stable Batch A/B/C truth with no unresolved contradictions
- status values: `not_ready` / `partial` / `ready`
- blocking risk: standalone promise resting on unstable product semantics

### 11.2 Governance readiness

- requirement: explicit governance model, queue logic, closeout authority, and sign-off rules
- evidence needed: documented governance model plus live operator use
- status values: `not_ready` / `partial` / `ready`
- blocking risk: enterprise misuse or governance drift

### 11.3 Tenant/admin readiness

- requirement: tenant roles, activation controls, audit export, retention and deletion controls
- evidence needed: explicit admin model and tested role boundaries
- status values: `not_ready` / `partial` / `ready`
- blocking risk: cannot safely operate outside Loep-specific assumptions

### 11.4 Security/privacy readiness

- requirement: privacy, dossier, monitoring, and access boundaries stay explicit and enforceable
- evidence needed: approved boundary artifacts and no unresolved incidents
- status values: `not_ready` / `partial` / `ready`
- blocking risk: standalone category becomes indefensible

### 11.5 Support readiness

- requirement: support path, escalation path, incident handling, and operator troubleshooting exist
- evidence needed: support model documented and exercised
- status values: `not_ready` / `partial` / `ready`
- blocking risk: founder-dependent operations

### 11.6 Onboarding readiness

- requirement: customer, HR operator, and manager onboarding are repeatable
- evidence needed: repeatable onboarding materials and tested rollout flow
- status values: `not_ready` / `partial` / `ready`
- blocking risk: product can only be explained live by insiders

### 11.7 Commercial readiness

- requirement: category, replacement narrative, packaging ladder, and claim rules are explicit
- evidence needed: buyer-safe materials that survive review and real conversation
- status values: `not_ready` / `partial` / `ready`
- blocking risk: category confusion

### 11.8 Evidence readiness

- requirement: live evidence exists across usage, review, sprawl/stale/no-progress, and HR chasing proxy
- evidence needed: multi-context live route evidence
- status values: `not_ready` / `partial` / `ready`
- blocking risk: standalone promise without operating proof

### 11.9 Category readiness

- requirement: Action Center is understandable as governed follow-through rather than workflow or survey tooling
- evidence needed: buyer/operator understanding confirmed in real review
- status values: `not_ready` / `partial` / `ready`
- blocking risk: misclassification into the wrong software category

### 11.10 Near-standalone positioning readiness

- requirement: Action Center can be explained as commercially distinct without being misread as a launched standalone product
- evidence needed: positioning gate passed, buyer-safe artifacts tested, sales boundaries understood
- status values: `not_ready` / `partial` / `ready`
- blocking risk: commercial overreach before product and operating maturity

## 12A. Gate To Near-Standalone Commercial Positioning

Near-standalone commercial positioning may not begin unless at least the following are true:

- category pitch has been exercised in real buyer or prospect conversations
- buyers can understand Action Center without workflow or project-management framing
- ICP and non-ICP are explicit and usable in qualification
- embedded Action Center buyer story works without founder-only explanation
- at least one buyer-facing artifact has passed red-team review under live commercial use
- sales can explain that Action Center is commercially distinct without claiming it is already a standalone product
- no unresolved privacy or governance incident undermines that positioning

## 12. Quantified Standalone Gate

Standalone launch consideration may not begin until at least the following are true:

- at least `3` live customer contexts
- at least `30` live route instances
- at least `2` approved route families live, or one route family explicitly excluded with rationale
- manager action completion above a defined threshold
- action review completion above a defined threshold
- stale / sprawl / repeated-no-progress rates below defined thresholds
- HR chasing proxy is interpretable and acceptable
- no unresolved `P0` / `P1` governance or privacy incidents
- buyer/operator interviews confirm boundedness is understood
- support/onboarding model has been tested without founder-only explanation
- the product can be explained without workflow or project-management framing

### 12.1 Interpretation rule

This gate proves only readiness for **standalone consideration**.

It does not prove:

- causal retention improvement
- causal exit reduction
- intervention effectiveness
- adoption proof in the broad commercial sense

Exact thresholds may be finalized later, but the gate must remain quantitatively hard before any standalone consideration begins.
